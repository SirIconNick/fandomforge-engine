# Character Tribute

A single-character focus edit. Celebrates one person's arc across their appearances — often across a full series or franchise. Structured around their emotional journey.

## Target numbers

| Metric | Target | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Median shot duration | **2.2s** | 1.2 – 4.0s |
| Cuts per minute | **27** | 18 – 38 |
| Beat-sync % | **55%** | 45 – 65% |
| Dominant transition | hard_cut (~50%) + dissolve (~30%) | |
| Dominant emotions | defiant, hopeful, somber | |

Sits between action and emotional. Faster than tender, slower than action. The character's arc drives pacing — fast during their triumphs, slower during their losses.

## Craft rules

### Structure — three-act is mandatory
Tribute edits live and die on narrative structure:

1. **Act 1 (0-35% of song)**: Introduce the character. Establishing shots, their habitat, what they fight for. Medium pace (~2-3s shots).
2. **Act 2 (35-70%)**: Escalation. Their struggle, obstacles, failures. Faster cuts (~1.5-2s).
3. **Act 3 (70-100%)**: Resolution or fall. Triumph shots, relationships paid off, or their death/loss. Pacing depends on emotional arc.

Shot counts per act should be roughly balanced — 33%/34%/33% is typical. Corpus shows slightly more in act 2 (~35%) for both tribute and character edits.

### Character-on-screen rule
- **Keep the character on screen for 70%+ of shots.** This is the defining difference from multifandom edits. Cutaways to villains, locations, other characters should feel like brief contextual flashes.
- **Reaction shots of the character** carry emotional beats. Their face during someone else's line of dialogue matters more than the speaker's face.
- **Hero shots at major beats** — low angle, backlit, iconic silhouette on drops and act transitions.

### Sync approach
- **Mood sync primary** — match emotional beats of song to arc of character. Saddest part of song → character's loss.
- **Beat-sync moderately** — 55%. Not as aggressive as action.
- **Internal sync** — character's gesture lands on musical accent.

### Transitions
- **Hard cuts within scenes** (during a single emotional beat).
- **Dissolves between acts** — signal the transition from setup to escalation to resolution.
- **Match cuts when possible** — character's sword-draw from one appearance → sword-draw from another. Powerful for legacy edits.

### Color + palette
- **Balanced grade** — not as dark as action, not as muted as emotional. ~0.28 luma, ~0.38 saturation.
- **Character-consistent coloring** — if the character wears red (Iron Man), red should be present in most shots. Use LUT to unify across sources.
- **Grade shifts per act** — act 3 often warmer/darker than act 1 to mark the emotional shift.

### Audio
- **Dialogue rips are gold here.** Quotes from the character — their iconic lines — layered over quiet musical passages become the edit's soul.
- **Song selection matters more than timing.** A song whose lyrics match the character's journey elevates the edit beyond craft.

## Example structure

Think an Iron Man tribute to "Legends Never Die":

- **0-45s (act 1):** Tony inventor origin, workshop, first suit, humor.
- **45s-2:00 (act 2):** Battles, losses, Yinsen, Obadiah, Civil War.
- **2:00-end (act 3):** Endgame — Thanos, the gauntlet, the snap. Last shot: "I am Iron Man."

That's the structure. The song carries the emotional arc; the editor picks shots that make the character's story line up with it.

## Common traps

- **Cutaway-itis.** Breaking from the character for too long dilutes the tribute. Stay on them.
- **Missing the arc.** Without clear 3-act structure it's a montage, not a tribute.
- **Overusing iconic lines.** One or two dialogue rips land hard; ten become noise.
- **Copy-paste pacing.** Sticking to a single cut rhythm across 3 minutes flattens the arc. Pacing should shift per act.
