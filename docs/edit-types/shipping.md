# Shipping / Romance

Romantic pairing edit. Soft, tender, dissolve-heavy. Celebrates a relationship arc — meet-cute through connection to intimacy or loss. The fandom-editing equivalent of a love letter.

## Target numbers

| Metric | Target | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Median shot duration | **3.2s** | 2.0 – 6.0s |
| Cuts per minute | **19** | 12 – 28 |
| Beat-sync % | **40%** | 25 – 55% |
| Dominant transition | dissolve (~45%) + hard_cut (~30%) | |
| Dominant emotions | tender, hopeful | |

Slower than tribute, faster than pure emotional. Dissolves dominate because emotional continuity is the signature. Reference shipping edits often sit 2-4 minutes long.

## Craft rules

### The pairing rule
- **Both partners must share screen time** — roughly 60-80% of shots feature the pairing together (two-shot) or one reacting to the other (reaction shot).
- **Solo shots** reserved for context (where they are, what they're doing when apart).
- **Two-shot compositions dominate** — balance in frame, shared light.
- **Eyeline matches** between reaction shots are the core visual language. Partner A looks screen-left; partner B appears looking screen-right in the next shot.

### Sync approach
- **Lyric sync primary.** This is the defining type for lyric sync. Song words should align with visual meaning — "I'll catch you when you fall" over a shot of one partner catching the other. Community guide: phrase sync works best in romance.
- **Mood sync constant.** Visual tone matches musical tone throughout.
- **Beat-sync light** (~40%). Romance edits shouldn't feel mechanically metered.

### Structure — arc matters
Shipping edits live and die on the relationship arc:

1. **Meet / early tension** (0-25%): First glances, accidental touches, establishing the attraction.
2. **Connection** (25-60%): Scenes where the pairing's bond forms. Dialogue rips, meaningful looks.
3. **Intimacy or conflict** (60-85%): Physical closeness (kisses, embraces) OR a major conflict/separation beat.
4. **Resolution** (85-100%): Together or apart, depending on canon and editor's choice.

Some ship edits (especially for canonically-dead pairings) invert the arc — start with the loss, spiral back through the relationship.

### Transitions
- **Dissolves dominate (~45%).** Emotional continuity across beats. Crossfades into new scenes.
- **Hard cuts (~30%)** during dialogue or confrontation sequences.
- **Fades** for major time jumps.
- **Soft wipes** rare — read as amateur in most contexts.
- **Match cuts** on gaze or gesture when possible (she closes her eyes → he closes his eyes, cross-source).

### Motion + framing
- **Gaze and eyeline over action.** The moment of eye contact is the shot.
- **Slow push-ins** on faces during emotional beats.
- **Shot-reverse-shot** during dialogue.
- **Over-the-shoulder shots** establish intimacy.
- **Avoid whip pans, speed ramps, and flash cuts** — they break the emotional register.

### Color + palette
- **Warm-soft grade.** Higher luma (~0.35) than action/emotional — romance lives in warmer light.
- **Moderate saturation** (~0.35). Not muted like pure emotional; not punchy like action.
- **Skin tones flattering** — lift warmth, hold contrast low.
- **Golden hour aesthetic** — if sources have sunset/sunrise footage, lean into it.

### Audio
- **Song selection is the whole game.** Pop ballads, indie folk, swelling synth tracks, pining acoustic. The lyrics must match the arc you're building.
- **Dialogue rips carry beats.** A "I love you" or "stay with me" over quiet musical passages is the emotional peak.
- **Scene audio low** — bring in kiss sounds, laughter, sighs at carefully-chosen moments only.
- **SFX minimal.** A single heartbeat sound under a slow-motion moment can work; anything more is too much.

## Common traps

- **Solo shots dominating.** If one partner is on screen alone for more than 30% of total runtime, it reads as a solo tribute with the other person guest-starring. Keep them together.
- **Action-editing a ship.** Beat-sync dominance kills emotional weight. Slow down.
- **Too much lip-sync.** Hitting every word's mouth-movement precision is exhausting. Use lip-sync once or twice, sparingly.
- **Grade too dark.** Romance asks for light. Dark grades read as grief, not love.
- **Bad song.** A song with no romantic content will not make an edit romantic no matter how you cut it. The lyrics carry half the work.

## When to use this type

- Your project is about a specific relationship arc between two characters.
- The song is a love song (or an unambiguous emotional song about a relationship).
- Your goal is to make the viewer feel the connection between the partners.
