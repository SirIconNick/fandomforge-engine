# Emotional / Tender

Opposite of action. Grief, loss, quiet triumph, vulnerability. The shots breathe, the cuts are earned, the song is mood not rhythm.

## Target numbers

| Metric | Target | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Median shot duration | **4.0s** | 2.5 – 8.0s |
| Cuts per minute | **15** | 8 – 22 |
| Beat-sync % | **35%** | 20 – 50% |
| Dominant transition | dissolve (~55%) | |
| Dominant emotions | somber, tender | |

## Craft rules

### Pacing
- Shots stay uncut for **5+ seconds** when the moment calls for it. The AMV community guide is clear: "scenes often remain uncut for five or more seconds" in dramatic/sentimental edits.
- Walter Murch's rule holds — "let shots run a little longer than what seems necessary." Emotion is the number one priority.
- When in doubt, hold longer. Audiences feel crowded by cuts during emotional peaks.

### Sync approach
- **Mood sync is primary.** Footage tone matches musical atmosphere. Melancholy scene over minor-key music, hopeful shot over major-key swell.
- **Internal sync next** — a tear that starts at exactly the musical swell. The cut doesn't have to be on the beat; the moment inside the shot does.
- **Beat-sync actively wrong.** Cutting on every beat breaks the emotional register. Aim for 20-50% beat-sync — enough that it feels musical, not mechanical.
- **Lyric sync works here** when the song is melodic. Cut on phrase endings, not word starts.

### Transitions
- **Crossfades and dissolves dominate.** Community guide: "crossfades are common" in drama/sentimental. ~55% dissolves vs ~25% hard cuts inverts the action ratio.
- **Fades to black** between major emotional beats. They function as breath marks.
- **Hard cuts reserve for the rare moment** you want to jar — grief-punch, shock, dissociation.

### Motion + framing
- **Eyelines over action.** Reaction shots carry the emotional beats. A held gaze is worth ten punches in an emotional edit.
- **Gentle motion only.** No whip pans. Speed ramps OK for slow-mo grief but ramping out of slow-mo breaks spell.
- **Shot-reverse-shot** between characters in conflict or connection — classic language.
- **Close-ups on faces** during peak emotional moments. Medium shots for context.

### Color + palette
- **Cool and muted.** Lower saturation (~0.30). Blue shadows, desaturated skin. The "melancholy" color language.
- **Warm highlights only for hope.** When the edit turns toward recovery or triumph, saturation and warmth come back.
- **Filmic grade** — teal/orange less aggressive than in action. Gentler contrast.

### Audio
- **Song-first but give it room.** No hard compressing; let the quiet passages stay quiet.
- **Dialogue cues** carry disproportionate weight here. A character line over the bridge of the song is often the edit's emotional peak.
- **Scene audio kept softer** (-24 to -28 dB) than in action. You don't want ambient noise fighting the music.
- **SFX minimal or absent.** A single impact on a climactic moment works; otherwise avoid.

## Common traps

- **Over-cutting.** Editors get nervous during long holds and cut too early. Trust the shot.
- **Beat-syncing.** Instinct for fandom editors coming from action — fight it.
- **Over-effecting.** Light leaks, chromatic aberration, film grain all read as trying-too-hard here. Less is more.
- **Saturation creep.** Emotional edits look weaker when colors scream. Desaturate more than feels comfortable.
