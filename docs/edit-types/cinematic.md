# Cinematic / Slow-Burn

Film-style editing applied to fandom footage. Long shots, motivated cuts, narrative priority. The edit reads like a short film, not a music video.

## Target numbers

| Metric | Target | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Median shot duration | **5.0s** | 3.0 – 10.0s |
| Cuts per minute | **12** | 6 – 18 |
| Beat-sync % | **25%** | 10 – 40% |
| Dominant transition | hard_cut (~70%) | |
| Dominant emotions | neutral, somber, hopeful | |

Baseline feature-film pacing is 4-6s average shot length. This type targets that zone. PT Anderson's *There Will Be Blood* hits 13.3s — the upper extreme. Most fandom edits should stay in the 3-10s range.

## Craft rules

### Pacing
- **Motivated cuts** — every cut must have a reason grounded in performance, narrative, or continuity. Music is not a reason.
- **Long holds** for performance moments. Let the actor's expression land.
- **Establish a baseline pace** and deviate from it intentionally. A sudden fast-cut sequence in an otherwise slow edit reads as intensification.
- The Walter Murch principle: emotion first, story second, rhythm third, eye-trace fourth, 2D plane fifth, 3D space sixth. Only when those higher values are satisfied should you cut.

### Sync approach
- **Internal sync only.** Actions within shots align with musical accents. Cuts do not need to align with music.
- **Beat-sync actively wrong.** Cutting on beats breaks the film illusion. Target 25% — essentially accidental alignment.
- **Mood sync background.** Song and footage should feel emotionally aligned even if the timing is independent.

### Transitions
- **Hard cuts dominate (~70%).** The majority of modern film uses hard cuts; only rarely fades or dissolves.
- **Fade to black** between scenes or major time jumps only.
- **Dissolves sparingly** — reserved for montage sequences or dream sequences.
- **No whip pans, no speed ramps, no flash cuts.** Those belong to music video language, not film language.

### Composition + framing
- **Shot-reverse-shot** during dialogue or confrontation.
- **Rule of thirds respected** in most framings.
- **Depth of field matters.** Shallow focus on subject, background soft.
- **Establishing shots** at the top of each sequence. Wide → medium → close-up classic progression.

### Color + palette
- **Filmic grade** — teal shadows, orange skin tones. The "Hollywood blockbuster" color language.
- **Medium luma** (~0.32) — darker than comedy, lighter than action.
- **Moderate saturation** (~0.40) — natural-leaning.
- **Grade per sequence** — flashbacks might be desaturated, present-day full color.

### Audio
- **Song can be instrumental** — orchestral scores, ambient tracks, film scores work.
- **Dialogue and diegetic audio** carry significant weight. Characters speaking is not to be muted.
- **SFX subtle** — one impact per major beat, not per cut.
- **Silence is allowed.** The song can drop out during dialogue.

## Structure
Three-act or five-act. Longer than most other types (3-7 minutes common).

1. **Setup** — introduce world, stakes, characters
2. **Inciting incident** — event that starts the story
3. **Rising action** — escalation
4. **Climax** — central confrontation
5. **Resolution** — aftermath

Each act can be a 45-90s segment. Transitions between acts are often fade-to-black or a beat of silence.

## When to use this type

- The source material has genuinely strong performances worth letting breathe.
- The song is a score or instrumental rather than a pop track.
- The goal is narrative, not rhythm.
- You have 4+ minutes to work with.

Don't pick this type if you want a hype multifandom compilation — it's fundamentally the wrong language for that.

## Common traps

- **Adding effects.** Chromatic aberration in a cinematic edit screams amateur. Keep it clean.
- **Music video thinking.** Resist cutting on beats. If you catch yourself doing it, you're in the wrong type.
- **Over-compressed pacing.** This is the ONE type where you should feel like you're under-cutting. Hold longer.
