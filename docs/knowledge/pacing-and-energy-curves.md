# Pacing and Energy Curves

The emotional journey of a multifandom edit isn't just about which clips you pick. It's about the shape of the energy over time. Flat energy = boring, no matter how good your clips are. All-high energy = exhausting, viewer tunes out.

## What is an energy curve?

Plot viewer-experienced intensity (0-100) across time. A good edit has a deliberate shape. A bad edit has a flat line or random peaks.

## The four canonical curves

### 1. The Classic Rise
```
Energy
 100 |                  ____    
  75 |             ____/    \__ 
  50 |        ____/            \
  25 |   ____/                  \___
   0 |__/                            \
     0%                              100%
```
Low intro, steady rise, peak at 75-85%, wind down. Safe, reliable, works for most edits.

### 2. The Double Drop
```
Energy
 100 |       ____       ____
  75 |      /    \     /    \___
  50 |     /      \___/         \
  25 |____/                      \___
   0 |                               \
     0%                              100%
```
Two distinct peaks (drops), with a valley between. Matches songs with two drops. More dynamic than single rise.

### 3. The Plateau
```
Energy
 100 |   ________________
  75 |  /                \
  50 | /                  \___
  25 |/                       \___
   0 |                            \
     0%                           100%
```
Rise fast, sustain high, gradual descent. Works for hype/chaos edits where you want maximum sustained energy. Risky — easy to exhaust the viewer.

### 4. The Descent
```
Energy
 100 |\
  75 | \____
  50 |      \____
  25 |           \____
   0 |                \____________
     0%                           100%
```
Start loud, quiet across the runtime. Unusual but powerful for grief/loss edits. The energy IS the descent.

## Micro-pacing: inside an act

Within each act, your cuts should have a micro-rhythm too:

### Beats-per-cut variation
```
Cut length pattern across an act:
2 beats → 1 beat → 2 beats → 0.5 beats → 1 beat → 2 beats
```
Not monotonous. Not purely accelerating. A conversation of cut lengths.

### The rule of 3-5
Sequences of 3-5 rapid cuts (on 1/8 or 1/16 beats) punctuated by a 2-beat hold shot. This is the rhythm your eye wants. More than 5 rapid cuts in a row = nausea. Fewer than 3 = no build.

## The valley principle

**Every edit needs at least one valley.**

A valley is a section where the energy drops — a still shot, a held gaze, a quiet vocal moment. Without valleys:
- Peaks don't feel like peaks (no contrast)
- Viewer can't breathe
- Edit feels exhausting

In a 2-minute edit, expect 2-3 valleys:
- One after the intro (settling in)
- One before the main drop (tension / anticipation)
- One after the main drop (release / comedown)

## Peak management

You have a finite number of peak moments. Spend them.

### Maximum peak count
- 30-60s edit: 1 major peak
- 60-120s edit: 1-2 major peaks
- 2-3min edit: 2-3 major peaks
- 3-5min edit: 3-4 major peaks

"Peak" = the biggest visual moment the audience will remember.

### Peak ingredients
A major peak usually combines:
1. The biggest shot you have (most iconic, most powerful)
2. The biggest beat moment (drop, vocal peak, bass hit)
3. Some treatment stacking (flash frame or flash-stack, bigger SFX impact)
4. A hold afterward (the "exhale")

## The opening 10 seconds

The first 10 seconds determine whether viewers keep watching. Rules:

1. **Don't open cold with your highest-energy content.** Save the peaks.
2. **Establish a visual hook by 3 seconds.** One memorable image.
3. **Hint at theme by 10 seconds.** The viewer should have a read on what this edit is about.
4. **Start at 60-75% energy if possible.** Lower than 50% loses attention on short-form platforms.

### Short-form specific (TikTok / Reels / Shorts)
- First 2 seconds are critical — viewers swipe fast
- Hook has to be in frame 1 or 2
- Often worth front-loading a peak moment (or a teaser of it) to catch attention

## The last 10 seconds

The last 10 seconds determine whether the edit is rewatched / shared.

### Landing patterns
- **Final peak, sharp cut to black** — classic, reliable
- **Peak, decelerate, hold on single shot** — emotional, lets the theme breathe
- **Echo the opening** — the last shot references the first, gives closure
- **Reveal** — the edit's thematic point crystallized in one final image

### What kills the ending
- Fading out on nothing — feels like "I ran out of song"
- Adding 3 seconds of credits you could have put earlier
- Letting the song outro play under visual filler
- A transition effect that distracts from the final image

## The energy curve as a tool

Before you cut a frame, sketch your energy curve. Literally. A curve from 0 to 100 over the duration.

Then at every decision point — "should I put a high-energy clip here?" — check the curve. If the curve says this point is a valley and you're reaching for a peak clip, you're fighting the edit.

### Energy curve template

```markdown
# Energy Curve — [Project]

Duration: 2:00

0:00 (15) ________________
0:10 (25) ________________
0:20 (35) ________________
0:30 (50) ________________
0:40 (70) ________________
0:45 (95) ██████████████ DROP
0:55 (60) ________________  (post-drop valley)
1:05 (75) ________________
1:15 (50) ________________  (valley)
1:30 (85) ________________
1:42 (100) ████████████████ SECOND DROP
1:52 (40) ________________  (release)
2:00 (20) ________________  (final image)
```

Draft the curve, THEN fill in the clips.

## Cutting rhythm by genre

Different genres of multifandom edit have distinct cut rhythm signatures:

### Action / hype
Average cut length: 0.3-0.8 seconds during peaks, 1-2 seconds during valleys. Never longer than 2 seconds.

### Emotional / character study
Average cut length: 0.8-2 seconds throughout. Shots held longer to let emotion land. 2-4 second holds at key moments.

### Hype drops / chaos
Sustained 0.2-0.5 second cuts during drops. Almost seizure pace. But with mandatory pre- and post-drop valleys at 1.5-3 second holds.

### Reflective / slow-build
Average cut length: 1.5-3 seconds. Sometimes 4-5 second holds for contemplative shots. Fast cuts only reserved for specific emphasis.

## Rhythmic diversity

Even within a genre, monotonous cut rhythm is death. Vary:

- **Length:** alternating short and long holds
- **Density:** sometimes dense cuts, sometimes sparse
- **Direction:** cuts driven by beat, by lyric, by motion, by visual idea
- **Intensity:** different shots require different cut lengths (a quiet face needs longer than an explosion)

## Sustaining attention on long-form

For 3-5 minute edits, structure longer:

### The 4-act long-form
- Act 1 (0-25%): setup + first crescendo
- Act 2 (25-50%): develop theme, second crescendo (usually main drop)
- Act 3 (50-75%): reversal or descent, quiet section, rebuild
- Act 4 (75-100%): climax + landing

Each act is like its own mini-edit with its own curve. The overall curve is the aggregate.

## The "watch it once" test

After your first assembly, watch the full edit ONCE. Not in scrub mode. Not pausing to fix things. All the way through.

- Where do your eyes get bored?
- Where do you feel surprised?
- Where do you feel moved?
- Where do you feel nothing?

The parts where you felt nothing are the parts your viewers will feel nothing on. Fix the curve there, even if the clips are good in isolation.
