# Shot Selection Theory

Picking the right shot for each moment is 70% of what makes a multifandom edit good. Great clips badly placed make a worse edit than average clips well-placed. Here's how to pick right.

## The role-first approach

Before you look at clips, decide what ROLE this moment plays. Common roles:

- **Hero shot** — iconic, character-defining. Shown at peaks.
- **Action beat** — a single decisive action (punch, draw, reveal). Lands on a beat.
- **Reaction shot** — a face processing something. Sits BETWEEN action beats.
- **Environment** — wide establishing. Sets mood, place, scale.
- **Detail** — close on an object or part. Hands, eyes, a scar. Builds intimacy.
- **Motion moment** — movement through frame (running, falling, flying). Drives momentum.
- **Held gaze** — character still, looking. Emotional breathing room.
- **Cut-on-action** — designed to end mid-movement, handing momentum to next clip.

For every slot in your shot list, define the role FIRST. Then look for the shot.

## The four-criterion filter

Every candidate shot is judged on:

### 1. Theme fit (1-5)
Does it reinforce your edit's one-sentence theme?

- 5 — Perfect thematic resonance, could be the theme's "logo shot"
- 4 — Strong theme support, clearly serves the point
- 3 — Thematically neutral — doesn't hurt, doesn't help
- 2 — Mildly dilutes the theme
- 1 — Actively contradicts the theme

### 2. Framing match (1-5)
Does it rhyme visually with the shots around it?

- 5 — Matches neighbors on at least 3 visual elements (motion direction, framing, composition, color, depth)
- 4 — Matches on 2 elements
- 3 — Matches on 1 element
- 2 — Doesn't match but the mismatch is covered by a transition
- 1 — Mismatched and jarring

### 3. Emotional weight (1-5)
Does it punch at the level this act demands?

- 5 — Iconic moment, maximum emotional impact
- 4 — Strong moment
- 3 — Solid workhorse shot
- 2 — Weak impact, filler-territory
- 1 — No emotional read

### 4. Beat fit (1-5)
Does the key visual action land on the beat?

- 5 — Visual action precisely aligns with downbeat or drop
- 4 — Aligns with a strong beat
- 3 — Lands somewhere in a beat range
- 2 — Needs significant timing adjustment (speed-ramp, trim)
- 1 — Doesn't fit the beat structure

**Total < 14/20 → reject.** Find something else.

## The iconic-shot tax

Every fandom has a handful of shots that everyone uses. They're iconic for a reason, but they carry a cost in multifandom edits:

- **Tony Stark's snap (Endgame)** — overused
- **"I am the one who knocks"** — overused
- **Luffy Gear 5 laugh** — currently trending, will be overused within months
- **Obi-Wan "you were my brother"** — overused
- **Anakin helmet pull in ROTS** — overused

If you use these, have a reason they're new in YOUR edit. Otherwise find the runner-up scene that hits the same emotion without the "I've seen this in 50 edits" tax.

## The 40% rule

No more than 40% of the shots in any edit should come from a single fandom. If Marvel is 60% of your shot list:

- You don't have a multifandom edit, you have a Marvel edit with guest stars
- Viewer's brain settles into Marvel, the other fandoms read as intrusions
- Cut back Marvel, find more clips from the others

The 40% rule applies to shot count AND screen time. A single 8-second Marvel shot counts more than three 1-second Harry Potter shots.

## The visual match tree

When cutting from shot A to shot B, shared elements make the cut feel intentional. Share at least one of:

### Composition
- **Subject position:** both in the left third, both center, both on rule-of-thirds intersection
- **Framing scale:** wide to wide, MCU to MCU, close-up to close-up
- **Depth:** both shallow DoF, both deep

### Motion
- **Motion direction:** both moving left-to-right, both pushing in, both falling
- **Speed match:** fast-paced action to fast-paced action, slow to slow
- **Direction of gaze:** both looking camera-left

### Color
- **Color temperature:** both warm or both cool
- **Dominant color:** both blue-heavy, both red-dominated
- **Contrast level:** both high-contrast, both muted

### Content
- **Action type:** both a punch, both a run, both a fall
- **Character scale in frame:** both full-body, both tight on face

If two shots share nothing, they'll feel wrong. Either find a better option or cover the break with a transition (whip pan, flash cut).

## The stillness principle

Great edits have more still shots than new editors expect. A still held shot (no motion, no cut) amidst rapid cutting punches harder than another cut.

### When to hold still
- Right after a drop (1-2 beats of a single shot)
- Mid-edit "breath" moment (quiet section, 2-4 beats on one shot)
- Thematic statement shot (a face that encapsulates the theme)
- Final image (2-3 seconds on one shot to close)

### Ratio
In a fast-paced 2-minute edit, expect 3-6 stillness moments. Each is 1-4 beats.

## The role of motion direction

Direction of motion across cuts can create meaning:

- **Same direction across multiple cuts** — sense of journey, progression, forward motion
- **Reverse direction** — conflict, opposition, "another side"
- **Converging motion (both toward center)** — collision, meeting, unity
- **Diverging motion (both away from center)** — separation, breaking

Designing motion direction intentionally across sections is advanced technique. It pays off.

## The depth variation rule

Don't cut wide → wide → wide → wide. Variety in shot scale keeps the edit visually rich.

Bad sequence: MCU → MCU → MCU → MCU
Good sequence: MCU → CU → wide → MCU → ECU → wide

Scales: ELS (extreme long shot) > LS (long shot) > MS (medium shot) > MCU (medium close-up) > CU (close-up) > ECU (extreme close-up)

Try to have at least 3 different shot scales per 30 seconds.

## The meaning-per-frame standard

Every frame should either:
1. Advance the theme, OR
2. Reinforce the emotion of this section, OR
3. Contribute to the rhythm/momentum

If a frame does none of those, it's filler. Cut it.

Hard standard: if you can remove a shot and the edit loses nothing, that shot should be gone. If you can remove a shot and the edit gets BETTER, you're right to cut it even if the clip is cool.

## Subtext and implication

The best multifandom moments come from juxtaposition creating new meaning.

Example: Cut Itachi's "Forgive me, Sasuke" forehead-poke immediately into Obi-Wan's "you were my brother, Anakin" — suddenly both scenes are ABOUT something they weren't individually about. That's the goal.

Look for pairs of clips where cutting them together creates a third meaning neither has alone. That's the craft.

## Red flags for shot selection

### "It's a cool shot"
Not a reason. Every shot in every movie is a "cool shot" to someone. Cool doesn't mean it belongs.

### "I wanted to include [fandom]"
Not a reason. If the fandom fits the theme, use it. If it doesn't, don't shoehorn.

### "This is everyone's favorite scene"
Not a reason. Popularity doesn't equal fit. Popularity plus fit = great. Popularity without fit = cliché.

### "It matches the lyrics"
Sometimes a reason. Literal lyric matching can become cheesy. Thematic lyric matching is better.

### "It's visually similar to the last shot"
A valid partial reason, combined with others. Visual similarity alone = nice aesthetic but doesn't mean the shot earns its slot thematically.

## The "second pass" rule

Assemble your rough cut. Then do a second pass specifically to KILL shots.

On second pass:
- Scrub to each shot
- Ask: "does this earn its slot?"
- If hesitant for 3+ seconds, cut it
- Go pick a better replacement

New editors preserve shots they love. Experienced editors kill them ruthlessly and find better ones. Your first pick is rarely your best pick.
