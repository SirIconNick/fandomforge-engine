# Theme Patterns

A theme is the one-sentence answer to "what is this edit about?" A strong theme is the spine of the edit. A weak theme is why your edit feels like a compilation.

## The one-sentence test

Your theme should be expressible in 12 words or fewer, in plain language, with an actual point.

**Bad themes:**
- "Heroes" (not a theme, a genre)
- "Cool action moments" (not a theme, a vibe)
- "Characters I love" (not a theme, a favorites list)
- "Everything beautiful from these 5 movies" (not a theme, a greatest hits)

**Good themes:**
- "What it costs to carry other people's hope"
- "The first time they chose to become someone worse"
- "People who kept going after they'd already lost"
- "The fathers who failed us"
- "Quiet moments before impossible violence"

## Proven theme frameworks

### 1. Cost
The price paid for a given power, role, or love.

Examples: "The cost of being the chosen one." "What being powerful took from them." "The cost of the throne."

Why it works: inherently bittersweet, creates contrast between achievement and loss.

### 2. Becoming
Who they were vs who they are now. Transformation arcs.

Examples: "From who they were to who they had to be." "Every hero starts afraid." "Becoming the thing they feared."

Why it works: character-forward, lets you use before/after juxtaposition.

### 3. Tribute
Honoring someone or something lost.

Examples: "For the ones who didn't make it." "Mentors we couldn't save." "Saying goodbye to a version of ourselves."

Why it works: emotionally universal, taps shared grief.

### 4. Defiance
Standing against a larger force.

Examples: "When the world said no, they said watch me." "No gods, no kings." "Kids fighting things they shouldn't have to fight."

Why it works: energizes the viewer, pairs naturally with hype songs.

### 5. Found Family
Connection built across difference.

Examples: "Strangers who became brothers." "Families you choose." "The crew that kept us alive."

Why it works: warm, ensemble-friendly, works across any fandom with ensemble casts.

### 6. Inheritance
Carrying someone's legacy forward.

Examples: "What the dead asked us to become." "The torch changes hands." "Remembering what you were given."

Why it works: thematic across mentor-student arcs; fits multifandom because every fandom has this beat.

### 7. Breaking Point
The moment they changed — usually for worse, sometimes for better.

Examples: "Everyone has a moment they snap." "When good people stop being good." "The line they won't cross until they have to."

Why it works: high-drama, puts a single moment in each fandom at the center.

### 8. Unison
Different heroes, same fight.

Examples: "All their wars are one war." "Different swords, same oath." "Everyone called to the same fire."

Why it works: central to the multifandom premise — literally the point of the edit is connection.

### 9. Quiet
The space between the action. Contemplative.

Examples: "What they do when no one's watching." "The stillness before." "Moments in between."

Why it works: counterintuitive for multifandom (usually action-forward), but creates a meditative edit that stands out.

### 10. Return
Coming back different.

Examples: "Home isn't where you left it." "The ones who went away and came back." "You can return, but not to who you were."

Why it works: structured inherently — departure, transformation, return — easy to plot.

## Theme density — how specific should you get?

Themes exist on a scale from abstract to specific.

### Too abstract
"Life is a journey." Too big. No edit can land this.

### Well-tuned
"The journeys we didn't choose." Specific enough to filter clips (which characters had involuntary journeys?), broad enough to pull from many fandoms.

### Too specific
"Tony Stark's specific arc across Iron Man 1-3 compared to Obi-Wan's Prequel Trilogy arc." That's a thesis. Turn it into an edit with a tighter theme: "The teacher who became the warrior."

Target the middle. Specific enough to exclude irrelevant clips, broad enough to fit multiple fandoms.

## Theme vs vibe

A vibe is a feeling ("sad", "hype"). A theme is a feeling plus a reason.

"Sad" is not a theme. "Grief that arrives before you're ready" is a theme. Both produce sad edits. The theme version has a shape.

Theme beats vibe because theme tells you what to EXCLUDE. A sad edit with no theme will grab any sad clip. A theme edit will reject sad clips that don't match the specific theme.

## Layering theme

Advanced edits can have a primary theme and a secondary theme. The secondary reveals as the edit progresses.

Example:
- Primary theme (what the viewer sees): "Heroes who knew they wouldn't survive"
- Secondary theme (what the viewer feels by the end): "And did it anyway"

The second half of the edit can reframe what you thought the first half was about. This is cinema-level storytelling.

## Testing a theme

Once you draft a theme, run it through these questions:

1. **Can I name 5+ scenes across 3+ fandoms that fit it?** If no, theme too narrow or too specific to one fandom.
2. **Is there a scene I reflexively want to use that DOESN'T fit this theme?** Good — means the theme is filtering. Keep it.
3. **Can I articulate the emotional arc the theme produces?** If the theme doesn't create emotional movement, it's a static premise, not a theme.
4. **Would a viewer who sees only the title understand this edit is about X?** If your title gives away the theme, that's fine for some edits. If the theme is a reveal, your title should NOT give it away.

## Theme and song relationship

A theme can come first (then you pick a song that fits it) or a song can come first (then you find a theme the song supports).

Either path is valid. What's not valid: having a song and no theme, or a theme and no song, and then just starting to cut. Lock both before you assemble.

## The one-sentence printout

At the top of every edit plan document, put your theme in a box:

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║  THEME                                            ║
║  The ones who taught us the thing that killed    ║
║  them.                                            ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════╝
```

Every time you face an editing decision, read the box. If the decision doesn't serve the box, reverse it.
