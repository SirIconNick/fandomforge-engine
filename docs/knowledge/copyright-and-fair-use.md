# Copyright and Fair Use

**This is not legal advice. Talk to an actual lawyer if you have a specific concern.** This doc is a plain-language overview of what multifandom editors need to know.

## The uncomfortable truth

Fan edits exist in a legal gray zone. Fair use is a case-by-case doctrine, not a blanket permission. Many edits technically violate copyright but have been tolerated by rights holders for decades. Some have been issued takedowns. Some creators have been sued. Most haven't.

Don't publish anything you'd be devastated to lose.

## The four fair use factors (US law)

Courts evaluate fair use claims on four factors:

### 1. Purpose and character of the use
- **Favors fair use:** nonprofit, educational, commentary, criticism, parody, transformative
- **Weighs against:** commercial, purely entertainment, not transformative

**What this means for you:**
- A fan edit that adds new meaning (new juxtaposition creating a new thematic point) is more transformative than one that just recombines clips for entertainment.
- Zero-ad, non-monetized posting is safer than monetized.
- Patreon-ed fan edits have been a target when creators start making real money.

### 2. Nature of the copyrighted work
- **Favors fair use:** factual / published
- **Weighs against:** creative / unpublished

Movies and TV shows are creative, which leans against fair use. This factor usually works against fan edits.

### 3. Amount and substantiality used
- **Favors fair use:** small portions
- **Weighs against:** large portions or "the heart" of the work

**What this means:**
- Using 2-3 seconds per scene is safer than using 30-second chunks.
- Using the most iconic/meaningful moment of a film (even if short) weighs against you more than using a random shot.

### 4. Effect on the market
- **Favors fair use:** doesn't substitute for the original
- **Weighs against:** could harm sales/licensing of the original

A fan edit doesn't replace watching the movie. But if a music artist has licensing deals with sync companies, your use of their song without a license could be considered market harm.

## What platforms do (in practice)

### YouTube
- **Content ID** flags audio (songs) and sometimes video. Matches trigger automated actions.
- Typical outcomes when a song match fires:
  - The rights holder claims the video (you keep it up, they take the ad revenue)
  - The rights holder blocks the video in some or all countries
  - The rights holder takes the video down entirely (with strike)
- Visual matches on copyrighted video are less common but happen
- **Three strikes = channel termination.** Strikes stay on your account for 90 days.

### TikTok
- Aggressive auto-detection
- Many songs are licensed platform-wide for "fan use" style content
- Some songs are specifically NOT licensed and will mute your video
- Visual content from movies/shows can be flagged but less consistently
- Bans are less common than YouTube, but muting and reach-throttling are

### Instagram Reels
- Similar to TikTok but harsher on music outside their licensed library
- If you use a song, use it from the Reels sound library when possible

### Twitter/X
- Minimal auto-detection
- DMCA takedowns happen but slower
- Generally the loosest of the major platforms for fan content

## Reducing your risk

These don't make you safe. They reduce the odds.

### Songs
1. **Use royalty-free or CC-licensed music when possible.** For a multifandom edit, this is usually not realistic — you're picking a specific song for emotional resonance. But know it's an option.
2. **Use songs from the TikTok/Reels sound library for those platforms.**
3. **For YouTube, accept that Content ID will probably claim it and you'll get no revenue.**
4. **Credit the song always.** In description, in end card, in pinned comment.

### Video sources
1. **Don't use the single most iconic moment at full duration.** Trim into the middle of a scene.
2. **Transform — don't just recombine.** Recolor, speed-ramp, reframe, add VFX. Visible transformation strengthens fair use.
3. **Credit your sources in description.** Not a legal shield but shows good faith.
4. **Don't upload anything from a pirated source explicitly (unreleased, leaked, cam-rip).** That's aggravated risk.

### Tone of your posting
1. **Don't claim ownership.** "I do not own any of this content" is NOT a magic phrase but its absence is worse.
2. **Don't monetize if possible.** Especially on longer or more prominent works.
3. **Don't sell fan edits.** Commercial use is much riskier.
4. **Don't use titles that make your edit feel like a substitute** ("FULL MOVIE RECUT" etc.).

## The "vidding" community

Long-standing fan-video tradition (see [Wikipedia: Vidding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vidding)) has produced thousands of transformative fan edits over decades, mostly without legal consequence. The Organization for Transformative Works and Archive of Our Own support this space legally and philosophically.

This community operates under an implicit understanding: non-commercial, transformative, attributed fan work is generally tolerated. It's not legal protection, but it's context.

## What DOES NOT protect you

These common beliefs are wrong:
- ❌ "No copyright infringement intended" / "All rights to owners" — zero legal effect
- ❌ "It's only X seconds per clip" — there's no magic length
- ❌ "I didn't monetize" — monetization affects the analysis, doesn't decide it
- ❌ "It's promotion for the original work" — rights holders decide their own promotion
- ❌ "Fan works are always fair use" — they're case-by-case, always
- ❌ "I credited the song" — credit doesn't equal license

## What TO DO every time you publish

1. Credit song (artist + title) in description
2. Credit visual sources (list the films/shows) in description
3. Don't claim ownership
4. Don't monetize if risk-averse
5. Accept that Content ID claims may happen
6. Be prepared to lose the video if a takedown comes — don't make something so precious you can't bear the loss
7. Keep your source project files so you can re-edit / re-upload if needed

## If you get a takedown

1. **Don't panic.** It's often automated.
2. **Read the notice carefully.** Is it Content ID or a formal DMCA?
3. **Content ID claims** can often be disputed if you have a strong fair use case. But disputes can escalate to strikes.
4. **DMCA takedowns with strike** — more serious. Three strikes = channel termination.
5. **Don't counter-notice unless you're willing to litigate.** A counter-notice under penalty of perjury asserts you have the legal right to post.
6. **Consult an actual lawyer** before any formal counter-notice.

## Bottom line

Fan edits are a long-tolerated tradition with meaningful legal uncertainty. Make them, share them, but don't stake your livelihood on any single piece. Credit everything. Don't monetize risky work. Accept that rights holders have the final say.
